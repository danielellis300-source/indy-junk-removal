Electronics Recycling in Indianapolis: What to Do With Old TVs and Computers

April 23, 2026 5 min read Indianapolis Junk Removal Co

Old electronics are one of the more legally regulated categories of household junk, and for good reason — they contain materials like lead, mercury, and cadmium that shouldn't end up in a landfill, alongside valuable recoverable materials like copper and rare earth metals.

Indiana's E-Waste Rules

Indiana's e-waste program restricts landfilling of many types of consumer electronics, which is why curbside trash collection generally won't take TVs, computers, and similar devices. This isn't unique to Indianapolis — it reflects a broader state-level push to keep e-waste out of general landfills and into proper recycling streams.

What Counts as E-Waste

  • Televisions (especially older CRT/tube models, which contain leaded glass)
  • Computers, laptops, and monitors
  • Printers and other computer peripherals
  • Small kitchen and household electronics in some cases
  • Cell phones and tablets

Where to Recycle Electronics in Indianapolis

  • Certified e-waste recyclers — the most reliable option; many accept a wide range of devices, sometimes for a small fee on older CRT TVs due to the glass recycling cost
  • Retailer take-back programs — some electronics and office supply retailers accept small electronics and batteries year-round
  • Community e-waste collection events — periodically held around Marion and surrounding counties; check current schedules before loading up a car
  • Manufacturer recycling programs — several computer and phone manufacturers offer mail-back recycling for their own products

Data Security First

Before recycling any computer, phone, or storage device, wipe personal data or physically remove the hard drive. A factory reset is a reasonable minimum for most people; for sensitive data, a certified data-destruction service or physically destroying the drive is safer.

Why Old TVs Are Especially Tricky

Older tube-style TVs are heavy, bulky, and contain leaded glass that requires special handling — this combination means many general haulers and even some e-waste events won't take them, or charge extra. If you've got an old console TV in a basement, this is often the single hardest item in a cleanout to place correctly.
